# Contributing

This is a curated list with a closed gate. Anyone can propose; the maintainer decides what lands. That filter is the point — an unfiltered list of tells becomes noise, and the opinionated shape is what makes this worth using over a generic word dump.

## What belongs here

A tell is a word, phrase, or construction that currently reads as AI-generated. Good submissions are specific, defensible in one line, and match on the *idea* rather than a single exact string.

Three things to include with any proposal:

1. **The tell** — the word, phrase, or construction.
2. **The category** — which group in `tells.md` it belongs to (filler & intensifier words, announcement pauses, false-contrast constructions, structural & rhythm tells, performance & theatre). If none fit, say so and propose one.
3. **A one-line reason** — why it reads as a tell.

You don't need to provide a rewrite or a "better" version. This project doesn't do fixes. It flags, names, and stops.

## How to propose

- **Issue** — fastest. Title it with the tell, fill in the three things above.
- **Pull request** — add your entry to the **Candidates** section of `tells.md`. The maintainer reviews and, if it holds up, promotes it into a category.

New tells always land in **Candidates** first. Promotion into a category is a separate, deliberate step.

## What gets rejected

- **Single common words with no cluster logic.** "The" is not a tell. Entries need to actually move the needle when they appear.
- **Tells that are really just bad writing.** Clichés and weak prose existed long before LLMs. This list is about patterns that read as *machine*-generated specifically.
- **Rewrites, scoring schemes, "humanizer" features.** Out of scope by design. This is detect-only. If you want that, the README links projects that do it.
- **Anything framed as a verdict.** The list reports what *reads as* a tell. It never claims a given text *was* written by AI. Submissions that assume otherwise get reframed or declined.

## A note on the philosophy

The list grows; it does not retire entries. The whole premise is that once a pattern reads as a tell, it stays legible as one — and that new ones keep arriving faster than feels reasonable. So the bar for adding is "does this currently read as AI to a careful reader," and the list gets longer over time. That is expected, not a problem to solve.

Keep entries terse. One line of reason each. The corpus earns its weight by being scannable, not exhaustive.
